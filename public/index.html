How Libor Works
By Toph Tucker Toph Tucker
September 3, 2015

The London Interbank Offered Rate, or Libor, is a global benchmark for the cost of borrowing. Every day, 16 banks say how much it might cost them to borrow money, in various currencies, for various lengths of time. In each category, the four highest and four lowest submitted figures are discarded, and the average of the remaining eight become that day’s Libor in that category.

Notice how Libor rates spiked during the 2008 financial crisis, and have been low lately.

Fig. 1 Mouse or touch to see banks’ submitted rates on a given date

The fact that Libor is computed as a “trimmed average” means that submitting a wildly high or low guess doesn’t move the figure. Even if you had total control over how one bank sets its rates, you couldn’t necessarily move Libor at all.

Fig. 2 Mouse or touch to change a hypothetical bank’s submitted rate

So when Tom Hayes allegedly set out to manipulate rates, he needed help. He allegedly offered favors to people in power, as well as counting on some degree of lazy herd mentality: some people just set their rates based on where they expect others to set their rates.

In this hypothetical toy model of how Libor works, you have total control over how one bank sets its rates, and some random partial influence on other banks, who will tend to follow you.

Fig. 3 Mouse or touch to change a hypothetical bank’s submitted rate; others may follow

Many financial contracts are based on Libor in some way; if you could move it, you could make millions.

As a qualitative example, imagine a simplified interest rate swap: a counterparty has agreed to pay you a fixed interest rate on some principal, and you have agreed to pay a floating interest rate on that principal, which moves with Libor.
