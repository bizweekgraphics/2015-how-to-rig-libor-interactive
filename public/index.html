How Libor Works
By Toph Tucker Toph Tucker
September 18, 2015

The London Interbank Offered Rate, or Libor, is a global benchmark for the cost of borrowing. Every day, a panel of banks say how much it might cost them to borrow money, in various currencies, for various lengths of time (or maturities). In each category, the highest quarter and lowest quarter of submitted figures are discarded, and the average of the remaining figures become that day’s Libor in that category.

For example, the yen 6-month Libor is shown below; it is at about a tenth of where it was during the late-2008 financial crisis, reflecting easier, safer borrowing.

Fig. 1 ICE Libor JPY 6 Month. Mouse or touch to see banks’ submitted rates on a given date

The fact that Libor is computed as a “trimmed average” (with outliers discarded) means that submitting a wildly high or low guess doesn’t move the figure. Even if you had total control over how one bank sets its rates, you couldn’t necessarily move Libor at all.

Fig. 2 Hypothetical. Mouse or touch to change a hypothetical bank’s submitted rate

So when trader Tom Hayes set out to manipulate Libor, he bribed traders at other banks with lunches and kickbacks. He also took advantage of the lazy herd mentality that leads bankers to just set rates according to where guidance predicted others to set rates.

In this hypothetical example, moving one bank’s submissions has some random partial influence over other banks’ submitted rates.

Many financial contracts are based on Libor in some way, so moving it can pay off. Imagine a simplified interest rate swap: a counterparty has agreed to pay you a fixed interest rate on some principal, and you have agreed to pay a floating interest rate on that principal, which moves with Libor.
